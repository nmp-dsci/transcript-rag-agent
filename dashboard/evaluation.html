Answer

Negative gearing changes (removing it for existing properties) significantly reduce investor borrowing capacity – in some scenarios borrowing can be almost double with negative gearing than without. This shifts investor demand away from existing builds toward new builds, where negative gearing (and depreciation) still apply. Capital gains tax discount reduction (from 50% to an inflation-indexed minimum 30%) applies to all assets, making long-term investment less lucrative. Long‑term property price trends are not explicitly stated, but the transcript implies: existing properties in investor‑heavy suburbs may see price declines as investors exit, while new‑build prices may be supported by redirected demand. Rental prices are expected to rise (the government’s modelling of a $2/week increase is ridiculed as unrealistic).

Winners: Developers/land holders (new‑build buyers get much more borrowing capacity), buyers agents focused on new builds, existing asset holders (grandfathered under old rules), short‑term rental operators (Airbnb), and company structures (flat 30% tax).
Losers: Younger generations trying to build wealth, small business owners (higher CGT on sale), first home buyers in high‑yield suburbs now competing with investors, and renters (higher rents).

Answer

The proposed changes restrict negative gearing to newly constructed housing; existing investment properties are grandfathered, while newly acquired established properties lose access [2]. Investors will only be able to offset rental losses against property income (not salary), with excess losses carried forward [1][4]. Capital losses remain offsettable only against capital gains [1]. In the long term, property prices are driven primarily by supply and demand, not tax benefits, and the changes are expected to have a minimal impact on prices overall [5][6]. A short‑term recalibration is possible, but markets tend to find equilibrium over time [6]. **Winners** include developers and sellers of off‑plan/new housing (due to continued negative gearing and depreciation) [3][7], existing landlords with grandfathered properties [3], homeowners (main residence CGT exemption remains) [5], and commercial property (still eligible) [7]. **Losers** are investors in established dwellings bought after the budget date [2]; these investors face reduced cash‑flow benefits and may pull back from certain suburbs [8]. Rent prices are expected to rise as costs are passed on to tenants [9]. Some investors may shift to shares or commercial property [10]. Overall, the changes aim to redirect investment toward new housing to increase supply [2][7].
